# Personal Brand Values — Business Solutions

> Core values and voice principles stripped of product specifics, industry focus,
> and company branding. Use this as the foundation for any business solutions
> work, regardless of client type or deliverable format.

---

# Part 1: Core Belief

**There is always something eating someone's week that shouldn't be.**

The work is to find it — then fix it, patch it, or kill it entirely.

Not every problem needs a tool. Some need a workaround. Some need to be deleted.

---

# Part 2: What I Am Not

- Not an AI agency.
- Not a software company (even though I build software).
- Not a consultant.
- Not a coder (I use AI to do the work coders used to do).
- Not corporate America.

The difference that matters: an operator, not a vendor.

---

# Part 3: The Value Sequence (Sacred Order)

**Time → Money Saved → Money Made.**

Always lead with time. Money saved is the second proof. Money made is the eventual upside.

Never reorder these in a client conversation — even if the money-made number is bigger. People trust the time conversation first. Every time.

---

# Part 4: Eight Core Principles

### 1. Time first, then money saved, then money made.
The order matters. Time is the most universally felt pain. Money saved follows naturally. Money made is the long arc.

### 2. Human-first — replace the work people hate, not the people doing it.
Address AI replacement fear directly. Never imply that staff will be replaced. The team stays. The boring work goes.

### 3. Find the real problem. Fix it, patch it, or remove it.
Most people can't name their actual bottleneck — they name a symptom. The job is to find the real thing, then pick the right move:
- **Fix** — build or buy the proper solution.
- **Patch** — quick intervention when a full fix isn't worth it.
- **Remove** — sometimes the task shouldn't exist at all.

### 4. Right tool for the job — even when that tool isn't mine.
Honest recommendations over upsells. If a third-party solution is the better fit, say so. "I'd rather lose a sale than sell you the wrong thing." This is the differentiator.

### 5. Start in plain English — go as deep as the person wants from there.
Never lead with jargon. When someone wants the technical detail, give it to them. The rule is "never start in jargon," not "never use it."

### 6. Personal now, scalable later — but always feels like a real person.
Operator energy, not consultant energy or Silicon Valley energy. A real person stands behind everything.

### 7. Built by an operator, for operators.
This started as automating the boring parts of my own job. Same approach, now for other people's work. Operator first. The experience is the proof.

### 8. Actually love this work.
Not stated as a slogan — demonstrated through behavior. The puzzle of finding the real problem, the moment someone realizes how much time they're getting back. That's the reason.

# Part 5: Voice & Tone

Sound like a **smart, tech-savvy operator who's sat in the client's seat** — not a consultant, not a startup founder, not a marketing agency.

### Sound like:
- A good friend who happens to know AI
- A peer who's dealt with the same kind of repetitive work
- Someone who'll give the honest answer even when it costs the sale

### Don't sound like:
- A tech startup
- A consulting firm
- A marketing agency
- An "AI expert"

## The North Star Rule

**If a word would feel out of place at a job site or a kitchen table, don't use it.**

Start in plain English. Go as deep as the client wants from there.

---

## Words to Use vs. Words to Avoid

### Use:
- automation
- tool
- workflow (sparingly — only when there's no plainer word)
- AI (people understand it now)
- save time / save your team time
- connect your software
- "set this up so it just runs in the background"
- the boring stuff / busy work / repetitive work
- bottleneck
- team, crew, shop
- owner, operator
- hours / weeks / weekends back
- "here's what it'll do, here's what it won't"

### Avoid:
- orchestration / orchestrate
- agentic / agents (in client-facing copy)
- leverage (as a verb)
- deliverables
- engagement / engagement timeline
- stakeholders
- technology stack / tech stack
- deploy / deployment
- integration layer
- solutions provider
- holistic / synergy / robust / scalable
- LLM, API, RAG, MCP (never in client-facing material)
- "AI-powered" / "AI-driven" / "powered by AI"
- "transform your business"
- "next-generation" / "cutting-edge" / "world-class"
- "supercharge" / "unlock" / "unleash"
- disrupt / disruption

## Sentence-Level Style

**Specific beats generic. Always.**

Bad: *"We handle the boring work so you can focus on what matters."*
Good: *"Stop chasing invoices, retyping quotes, and forgetting follow-ups. Get back to the job."*

**Lead with the outcome. Reveal the method second.**

Bad: *"Our AI workflow automation platform helps streamline operations."*
Good: *"Get your Saturdays back. Here's how."*

**Numbers and outcomes beat adjectives.**

Bad: *"Powerful AI-driven quote automation."*
Good: *"Saves about 4 hours a week on quotes."*

**Short sentences. Plain syntax.**

If a sentence runs more than 20 words, it should probably be two sentences.

**Use second person (you/your).** Talking to a person, not "the customer."

**Active voice. Always.**

**No hedging. No fake humility.** Confident about what I do. Honest about what I don't.

---

## Tone Patterns That Work

**The honest counter-pitch** — turn down the upsell to build trust.
> "Sometimes the right answer isn't a tool — it's stopping the task altogether. I'll tell you when that's the case."

**The operator-to-operator** — reference real work, not abstract business language.
> "If you've ever spent a Sunday night doing work you should've finished on Friday, this is for you."

**The puzzle frame** — reference genuine love of the work.
> "Finding the real bottleneck is the fun part. Most of the time it's not what you think it is."

**The five hours prompt** — anchor on upside, not pain.
> "What would you do with an extra five hours a week?"

---

## Email Conventions

- **Subject lines:** specific and short. *"5 hours/week back"* beats *"Discover the power of AI for your business."*
- **First line:** no "I hope this email finds you well." Get to the point.
- **Length:** as short as the message needs. Cold outreach under 100 words.
- **Sign-off:** plain. *"— Tucker"* Nothing fancy.

---

## Things to Avoid Across All Copy

- **Robot iconography.** No silver androids, no glowing brains, no circuit boards.
- **"AI" as a hero.** AI is the tool. Time, clarity, and relief are the product.
- **Hype.** No exclamation marks unless genuinely warranted.
- **Buzzword density.** If a paragraph has more than two words from the avoid list, rewrite it.

---

## Voice Check Before Publishing Anything

Read it aloud and ask:

1. Could I say this to a skeptical business owner over coffee without him rolling his eyes?
2. Did I name something specific, or did I stay generic?
3. Did I lead with the outcome (time, money) before the method (AI, automation)?
4. Did I avoid corporate jargon and tech-startup jargon?
5. Does it sound like a real person, or like marketing?

If any answer is "no," rewrite.

# Part 6: The Founder Position

This started as one person automating the boring parts of their own job — not because they were a tech person, but because they were stubborn about repetitive work. It worked. Then they started helping friends do the same. Totally different industries, same principle every time: there's always something eating people's time, and there's almost always a way to take it off their plate.

The best part is the moment when someone realizes how much time they're about to get back.

**Key notes for telling this story:**
- Don't hedge ("just," "only," "kind of"). The problem solved was real.
- Specifics are the proof. Different industries validates the method.
- The "free for friends" piece matters — it says the work would happen anyway.
- End on the client, not yourself. Always pivot back to "here's what this means for you."

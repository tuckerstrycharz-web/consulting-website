Methodology

How to Diagnose a Problem

Before you automate anything, you have to understand it. Here's the exact process we use to map a business problem before writing a single line of code.

Step one

The Five Essential Elements

Every business process — no matter how complex — can be broken into five parts. Nail all five and you'll know exactly what to automate, what to leave alone, and what's hiding under the surface.

01
The Trigger
What event starts the process? An email, a form submission, a calendar event, a threshold crossed in a report.
02
The Inputs
What data, documents, or context does the process need to run? Where does that information come from right now?
03
The Steps
The discrete actions someone takes — in order — to move from trigger to outcome. Every handoff, every tool, every click.
04
The Decision Points
Where does a human apply judgment? These are the moments that determine whether we automate or augment.
05
The Outcome
What does "done" look like? How do you know the process succeeded — and what happens when it doesn't?
Step two

How Do You Discover These Elements?

You won't find the real process in a Confluence doc or a slide deck. You find it by talking to people and watching work happen in real time.

Method 01
The Interview Method
Sit down with the person who actually does the work — not their manager, not the operations lead. Ask them to walk you through the last time they did this task, step by step.
Ask: "Walk me through the last time you did this."
Follow up: "What did you do right before that?" and "What happens next?"
Probe for exceptions: "When does this process break down?"
Ask about the tools they use at each step — every app, spreadsheet, and inbox.
Method 02
The Observation Method
When possible, watch the process happen. Ask someone to share their screen and do a real task while you take notes. You'll catch steps they forgot to mention in the interview — because they're automatic to them.
Request a screen-share session during an actual work task — not a demo.
Don't interrupt; take notes on every tool, tab, and copy-paste moment.
Ask clarifying questions after, not during.
Note the time stamps — understand how long each step actually takes.
Step three

Understanding Inputs

Inputs are the raw material of any process. You need to know exactly what enters the workflow, in what format, and from where — because that determines what's automatable.

Step five

Defining the Outcome

Most automation projects fail because no one agreed on what "working" looks like before they started. Define success before you build anything.

What does "done" look like?
Be specific. "Invoice processed" is not a definition. "Invoice approved, payment scheduled, confirmation sent to vendor, and record filed in NetSuite" is a definition you can test against.
How do you measure success?
Pick a number before you build. Processing time per invoice, error rate, hours per week saved, cost per transaction. If you can't measure it, you can't prove it worked.
What does failure look like?
Define the failure modes explicitly. A double-payment is worse than a delayed one. A missed invoice due date has different consequences than a misrouted approval. Know the blast radius before you automate.
What's out of scope?
Write down what this automation does not do. This protects you from scope creep and gives the team a clear boundary to work within. "This does not handle credit notes or multi-currency invoices" is a complete sentence.
Talk to the people doing the work, not just their managers.
Why this matters
Managers describe how the process is supposed to work. Workers describe how it actually works. The gap between those two things is where most automation projects derail — and where the best opportunities hide.
Who to talk to
The newest person on the team (they actually read the docs). The longest-tenured person (they know every exception). And the person who complains most — they've already mapped the friction for you.
How to assign ownership
For every step in your process map, write a name — not a role. "Finance team" owns nothing. Sarah in AP owns the three-way PO match. Hold that level of specificity or the automation will have no one to escalate to when it breaks.
Step six

Document Everything

Your documentation has two jobs: describe the happy path in enough detail that a machine can follow it, and catalog every exception so you know what falls outside the automation's scope.

Processes
The happy path — how the work flows when everything goes right. Write this in enough detail that someone who's never done the job could follow it.
→
Every step, in sequence, with the tool used at each step
→
Who owns each step — by name, not by title
→
Average time each step takes in the real world
→
Where data enters and exits the process
→
What the final state looks like — in every system of record
Outliers
Edge cases, exceptions, and one-offs. These are the things that make automation hard — and the things you must explicitly decide whether to handle or exclude.
⚠
Requests that arrive in an unexpected format
⚠
High-value or high-risk transactions requiring extra review
⚠
Vendor or customer accounts with special handling rules
⚠
Processes that split or merge partway through
⚠
Regulatory or compliance constraints on specific data types
The biggest threat to your project
Scope creep.

It is the biggest issue in this business and it will become the bottleneck. You map one process, and three more appear. Someone asks "while we're at it, could it also…" and suddenly a four-week project is a six-month one. The diagnosis phase is where you draw the fence — and hold it.

Warning sign
"While we're at it..."
Any sentence that starts with these four words is a scope change in disguise. Log it. Schedule it. Do not build it now.
Warning sign
The "one more exception"
Each exception you add to the automation makes it exponentially harder to test and maintain. Handle the 80% case first. Build the exceptions in phase two — after you've proven the core works.
Warning sign
Undefined ownership
If no one on the client side owns the process being automated, the scope will expand to fill the vacuum. Every automation needs a named human owner who can say no.
The checklist

The Typical Process

This is the sequence we follow on every new engagement. Each step produces a deliverable we can both look at before moving forward.

01
Kickoff interview with the process owner
30 minutes. We want to understand the business context, the pain point, and what a successful outcome means to them — in their words.
Day 1
02
Shadow sessions with the people doing the work
One to three sessions, 45–60 minutes each. Screen-shares during real tasks. We take notes; they work normally.
Days 2–4
03
Draft the process map
Every step, every owner, every tool. Trigger, inputs, steps, decision points, and outcome — all five elements accounted for.
Day 5
04
Walk through the map with the team
We present the process map back to the people who live it. They correct us. We update. We repeat until they say "yes, that's actually how it works."
Day 6
05
Catalog the exceptions and edge cases
We ask explicitly: "When does this process break?" and document every answer. Then we make explicit decisions about what's in scope versus what's phase two.
Day 7
06
Define the success metric
One number we're optimizing for — agreed on by both sides. Time saved, error rate reduced, volume processed. We write it down and sign off before building anything.
Day 7
07
Scope document delivered
A one-page document that covers: what we're building, what we're not building, who owns what, and how we'll measure success. This is the contract that prevents scope creep.
Day 8
08
Green light to build
Both sides sign off on the scope document. We start building. The diagnosis phase is done.
